Microplastics have emerged as one of the most persistent and challenging contaminants in modern drinking water systems. Due to their small size, chemical stability, and diverse morphology, microplastic particles often evade conventional treatment processes such as filtration, sedimentation, and adsorption. Beyond their physical presence, microplastics can act as carriers for toxic chemicals and microbial contaminants, raising long-term environmental and public health concerns.

This project investigates the feasibility of **high-voltage non-thermal plasma** as an advanced, non-conventional technique for treating microplastic-contaminated water. The experimental approach is based on generating **low-pressure non-thermal plasma using high-voltage electrical excitation**, and exposing microplastic-containing water samples to the plasma under controlled conditions. Plasma discharges are produced using sinusoidal and square-wave high-voltage sources in the range of approximately 8‚Äì15 kV, allowing comparison of discharge behavior and treatment effectiveness across different excitation modes.

Water samples containing microplastics are prepared through controlled aging of tap water in single-use plastic containers, creating a representative laboratory-scale system for investigation. The work is intentionally exploratory and research-oriented, aimed at identifying trends, limitations, and promising operating windows rather than claiming immediate large-scale applicability.

Overall, this project seeks to contribute experimental insight into the emerging field of **plasma-assisted water purification**, with specific focus on microplastic remediation. The outcomes are expected to inform future optimisation studies, hybrid treatment strategies, and the broader discussion on sustainable, next-generation water treatment technologies.
